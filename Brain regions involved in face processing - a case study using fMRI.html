Brain regions involved in face processing - a case study using fMRI

Agata Kozioł

  1. Introduction

Faces play an undeniable role in the environment, as their perception does not only contribute to social life but also is crucial in survival from an evolutionary perspective (Solso, 2000). In nature, judgments about encountered creatures are made based on their mimicry. Animals’ facial expressions, as well as body postures, reveal a lot about their friendly or threatening intentions (Darwin, 1872). While seeing a human, the face is our primary interest. This is a basis for our early decision whether we want to get to know somebody or not (Ekman et al., 1980).

Therefore, there is no surprise that there are cortical areas in the brain specialized for processing faces. Traditionally, the three regions responsible for face processing are the fusiform face area (FFA) (Kanwisher et al., 1997) located on the ventral temporal lobe, the occipital face area (OFA) (Gautier et al., 2000) on the lateral inferior occipital lobe and posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS) (Puce et al., 1998), which activate more strongly to faces than objects (Haxby et al. 2000; Pascalis et al., 2011). Although their exact role may still be questionable, the FFA is believed to be responsive to facial configuration, the OFA to particular elements of a face, while the pSTS is sensitive to dynamic changes, like processing facial expressions (Gschwind et al., 2012). However, face perception is a much complex process and it does not rely solely on those three areas. There are other regions identified, including the amygdala, anterior temporal cortex, posterior cingulate and prefrontal areas showing activation to faces that together may form a system for social and affective processing (Gobbini & Haxby, 2007; Gschwind et al., 2012).

Fusiform face area (FFA)

One breakthrough in face perception studies was a result of research by Kanwisher et al. (1997). He designed an fMRI experiment measuring brain response to faces in comparison to viewing objects. The results highlighted the role of a region in the fusiform gyrus identified as the fusiform face area (FFA). The fusiform gyrus was already considered essential in face processing due to research on prosopagnosia revealing that it is caused by damage in that region (Damasio, Damasio & Van Hoesen, 1982; Weiner & Grill-Spector, 2013). However, it is the work of Kanwisher that contributed the most to defining the fusiform face area (Bruce & Young, 2012). In a series of later studies on the FFA (Tong et al., 2000) conducted to explore its importance, equally strong activation was observed for: cat, cartoon and human face; entire human face and face without visible eyes; front and profile perspective of a human face. However, the FFA was responding weakly to eyes depicted alone and faces moving away. Those findings suggest the role of the FFA in recognizing face-like structure and it appears to work as a face detection mechanism (Tong et al., 2000).

Occipital face area (OFA)

        The occipital face area, the next region participating in face perception, traditionally is described to be responsible for detecting particular parts of a face, e.g. eyes, nose and mouth. In a study by Rotshtein et al. (2005), it was found that the OFA responds to physical changes in a face regardless of whether they result in creating another identity. This finding is consistent with the idea that the OFA activates at an early stage of visual processing, suggesting its priority in a hierarchical network of face perception (Pitcher, Walsh & Duchaine, 2011). However, a recent study by Eick et al. (2020) questions those assumptions, as it was indicated that the OFA takes part in the higher face processing, which is contradictory to the association of the OFA only with the low-level processing presented in multiple elaborations (Bruce and Young 1986; Haxby et al. 2000). Eick et al. (2020) applied TMS stimulation to the OFA during encoding of identity-related semantic information in order to observe further recalling abilities. The following reduction of correct biographical facts recall indicates the possible role of the OFA in semantic knowledge acquisition, which is supported by similar findings in encoding face identity information (Amado et al. 2018; Ambrus et al. 2019). Therefore, there is an indication that the OFA takes part in the higher-level face processing, as it seems to be responsible for representing identity-related information regarding a familiar face, as well as associating such information with a particular face.

Posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS)

        The superior temporal sulcus is believed to be a multifunctional area that is responsible for a variety of features, including the theory of mind (Saxe, 2006), audiovisual integration (Amedi et al., 2005), speech processing (Price, 2000), biological motion (Puce & Perrett, 2003) and also face perception (Iidaka, 2014). In a series of experiments, Hein and Knight (2008) identified particular cognitive abilities as being processed by either the anterior or posterior part of the STS. In order to distinguish motion processing from face perception, Hein and Knight (2008) in their study included static and scrambled face pictures. The activation was observed in the posterior subregion of the STS, and since this part was also established to be involved in motion processing, this is consistent with the finding that the STS is sensitive to eye gaze (Pelphrey et al., 2005). It is not clear why the STS serves so many functions, however, there is a suggestion that it is determined by network interaction with other brain areas, for example, the middle temporal gyrus (MTG), and those connections should be further explored in order to understand higher cognitive functions like face processing (Hein & Knight, 2008). In conclusion, although the functioning of the STS is not fully understood, there are studies suggesting the role of the pSTS in dynamic aspects of a face, since it shows greater activation to dynamic than static faces, which is not registered in neither FFA nor OFA (Pitcher et al., 2020).

Aim of the present study

Face perception seems to rely on three core regions: FFA, OFA and pSTS (Pascalis et al., 2011). However, those are not the only structures activated during face processing, which suggests that the corresponding brain network is much more complex. There was an activation observed in, i.a., the right middle temporal gyrus (Kanwisher, McDermott, Chun, 1997; Kitada et al., 2010, 2013), inferior frontal gyrus (Kitada et al., 2010, 2013), anterior STS (Iidaka, 2014) and anterior temporal pole (ATL) (Gobbini and Haxby 2007). The goal of this research is to explore fundamental neural mechanisms of face processing and to compare them with contemporary knowledge. Since recent findings brought a little confusion to so-thought defined network of face processing (Eick et al., 2020; Rapcsak 2019), there is a need to reevaluate the idea and further explore the neural basis of face perception.

Our hypothesis is that during viewing pictures of faces, there will be significant activation in areas connected to face perception, i.e. fusiform gyrus, the inferior occipital gyrus and the posterior superior temporal sulcus.  

  1. Methods

2.1. Participant

The participant was a 22-year-old woman. She was right-handed and in good health.

 

2.2. Design and materials

The participant’s task was to passively view multiple pictures. In order to capture face processing, the main condition was pictures of faces and the other, pictures of houses, worked as a control parameter. As a baseline between those two conditions, a fixation cross was presented. The experiment was a block design and the participant was presented with pictures of multiple faces and houses separated by the fixation cross. For more detailed information see Figure 1.

2.3. Image acquisition and analysis

The participant was scanned using 3T scanner Prisma, with the following parameters: TR = 1500 ms, TE = 30ms, flip angle = 90, FOV = 192×192 mm, matrix 64×64.

        fMRI data were analyzed using SPM12 (https://www.fil.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm/software/spm12/), xjview (https://www.alivelearn.net/xjview/) and visualized with MRIcroGL (https://www.nitrc.org/projects/mricrogl).

        Data were preprocessed accordingly with the following stages. A slice-timing correction was applied, adjusting the differences in acquisition times using a TR = 1500 ms and number of slices = 48. Data were motion-corrected and co-registered, then normalized onto a common brain space (MNI template) establishing voxel size to 2x2x2 mm3, and smoothing was applied using a Gaussian filter (full width half maximum = 5 mm). Functional image analyses were conducted with SPM12 and used FWE-corrected p thresholds, at p < 0.05. There were four contrasts identified: Faces vs Houses (subtracting house images from face images), Houses vs Faces (subtracting face images from house images), Faces vs Cross (subtracting fixation cross from face images), and Houses vs Cross (subtracting fixation cross from house images). In the analysis only Faces vs Houses and Houses vs Faces were taken into consideration, as the main focus of the experiment was face processing.

  1. Results

To investigate the face perception we relied on Faces vs Houses contrast and to evaluate the object processing, we focused on Houses vs Faces. Coordinates of peaks for the contrasts are presented in Table 1. Significant activations (p < 0.05, FWE corrected at voxel level) in particular brain regions were observed as follows. The contrast for the main effect of faces (Faces vs Houses) revealed activity in regions traditionally associated with face perception: fusiform gyrus (FFA) and inferior occipital gyrus (OFA), as we predicted in our hypothesis, however, the activation was observed as well in the right middle temporal gyrus (Figure 2). Activation of the fusiform gyrus in the right hemisphere was stronger than in the left since the right side is represented by two clusters (k = 64 and 51).  In Houses vs Faces contrast, the activation was observed in the middle occipital gyrus, right calcarine, right superior occipital gyrus and left fusiform gyrus (Figure 3).

  1. Discussion

4. 1. Faces vs Houses

        Traditionally, the core system of face processing is described to be FFA, OFA, and pSTS (Pascalis et al., 2011). In the study, there was significant activation in the fusiform gyrus and inferior occipital gyrus, however, not only explicit activation in the pSTS was not observed, but there was also an involvement of the middle temporal gyrus (MTG) in the task.

4.1.1. FFA

        Fusiform gyrus was more active in the right hemisphere, which is consistent with several findings in which this side was more responsive during face perception (Kanwisher et al., 1997; Yovel, Tambini, & Brandman, 2008; Bruce, & Young, 2012). It could be accounted for the person to person variability in the cerebral asymmetry (Yovel, Tambini, & Brandman, 2008), which would be compatible with the locus of damage in prosopagnosia. Most patients suffer from bilateral injuries, but there is a minority affected only by the right fusiform gyrus damage (Fox, Iaria & Barton, 2008).

Fusiform gyrus was highly responsive during viewing pictures of faces, which is in accordance with contemporary knowledge about the FFA. This region is believed to be responsible for detecting the overall face category, i.e. configuration of eyes, nose and mouth that together shape a face. FFA would then work as a face detection system (Tong et al., 2000). While looking at a picture one can detect a global face pattern, therefore activation of the FFA is expected. High activation of the FFA in the study (Figure 4a) indicates that our results are compatible with what we now know about this region.

4.1.2. OFA

Activation in the inferior occipital gyrus was observed bilaterally, in the right (k = 58) and in the left (k = 57) side. This region is thought to be responsible for the perception of particular facial features, like eyes, nose and mouth (Rotshtein et al., 2005). OFA is traditionally associated with the early processing of facial elements. Therefore, in a picture, where those features can be easily recognized and detected, there is a desire to observe activation in the inferior occipital gyrus. In the study, this expectation was fulfilled (Figure 4b), therefore our results are consistent with contemporary assumptions regarding the OFA.

4.1.3. Posterior STS

        Although the posterior superior temporal sulcus is thought to be one of the main areas responsible for facial recognition (Iidaka, 2014), it is not explicitly active during our experiment. Actually, the responsiveness of the pSTS during face perception tasks is not so usual (Kanwisher & Yovel, 2009). Although regions like FFA can be observed in all neurotypical subjects, the pSTS activation is observed in only half (Kanwisher et al., 1997) to three quarters (Yovel & Kanwisher, 2005). In our experiment, the lack of explicit pSTS activation could be accounted for the character of the task, which was viewing static face pictures and pSTS is active during processing dynamic aspects of a face, i.e. eye gaze and facial expressions (Haxby et al., 2000). Moreover, by delivering TBS over the right pSTS, there was a reduction observed in response to dynamic, but not static faces (Pitcher, Duchaine & Walsh, 2014). Therefore no explicit activation of the pSTS during our task should not be worrying.

4.1.4. MTG

        The middle temporal gyrus is bounded by the superior temporal sulcus (STS) from the superior temporal gyrus (STG) dorsally and by the inferior temporal sulcus (ITS) from the inferior temporal gyrus (ITG) ventrally. Activation of the MTG has been detected during multiple tasks, i.a. language processing, motion observation, or deductive reasoning (Xu et al., 2015).

Although high activation in the right middle temporal gyrus (MTG) during face processing tasks is not typical, it has been observed (Kanwisher, McDermott, Chun, 1997; Kitada et al., 2010). In a study by Klamer et al. (2017), the MTG region adjacent to the pSTS was stronger activated to emotional rather than neural dynamic facial expressions, and the MTG activation positively correlated with the neuroticism score of the subject. In another study by Vartanian et al. (2013), participants who on average gave higher ratings to the face attractiveness also had significantly higher activation exclusively in the MTG. This finding does not indicate the role of the MTG in assessing attractiveness, however, there is a possibility of another explanation for these results, e.g. individual variance in emotional processing while observing faces. MTG together with pSTS is usually associated with dynamic face processing and there were studies in which the MTG displayed an enhanced signal for dynamic emotional (disgusted/happy/sad) facial expressions in comparison to static (Trautmann et al., 2009; Foley et al., 2012). Importantly, in a study by Sato et al. (2004), the MTG showed higher activation during fearful and happy dynamic facial expressions than during either of the neutral dynamic or emotional static control stimuli. Therefore, there is a connection between face processing and the MTG responsiveness, and

4.2. Houses vs Faces

        During perception of houses, there was significant activation in the bilateral middle occipital gyrus (MOG), with the favoritism of the left hemisphere. Traditionally, object recognition associated is with the lateral occipital complex (LOC) located on the lateral bank of the fusiform gyrus extending into the occipito-temporal sulcus (Grill-Spector, Kourtzi & Kanwisher, 2001). This region is responsive while viewing pictures of objects (Malach et al. 1995), and there were studies indicating its role in the higher-level shape perception (Kourtzi & Kanwisher, 2001). There is evidence that lesions in the fusiform and occipito-temporal junction (Farah, Hammond, Mehta, & Ratcliff, 1989; Damasio, 1990), as well as repetitive transcranial stimulation (Stewart, Meyer, Frith, & Rothwell, 2001) interference with recognition processes, indicating their role in object perception (Grill-Spector, Kourtzi & Kanwisher, 2001). Activation in the occipital cortex was also registered during viewing animal pictures (Chao, Haxby, et al., 1999; Martin et al., 1996), which may suggest the responsiveness of the earliest visual processing regions of the medial occipital cortex (Ishai, Ungerleider, Martin & Haxby, 2000).

        During a house perception task in our experiment, activation of the left fusiform gyrus, traditionally associated with face processing, was also observed. There are instances of the fusiform gyrus activation during object perception tasks (McCarthy, Puce, Gore & Allison, 1997; Grill-Spector, 2003), and there are suggestions to link it with recognition abilities (Bar et al., 2001). Due to the similar composition of houses and faces (i.e. pattern of eyes-windows, mouth-door), the activation of the fusiform gyrus would also be consistent with the observation that the FFA responds to broad face-like categories (Tong et al., 2000).

5.  Conclusion

        Face perception is one of the fundamental human abilities, it is, i.a., a basis of social interactions (Avery et al., 2016). Neural activity during face viewing tasks has been a target of extensive research (e.g. Kanwisher et al., 1997; 2009; Haxby et al., 2000) and is still studied today (Eick et al., 2020). In our experiment, we observed activation in the bilateral fusiform gyrus, the inferior occipital gyrus and the right middle temporal gyrus. The results are consistent with contemporary knowledge about face processing stating that the FFA is responsive to overall face pattern and the OFA detects particular facial elements (Pascalis et al., 2011). Activation of the right MTG can be accounted for by emotional dynamic processing of facial stimuli (Sato et al., 2004).

        Viewing pictures of houses in our experiment activated, i.a., bilateral middle occipital gyrus and left fusiform gyrus. Activation in the occipital area could be evoked by visual processes or shape recognition (Ishai, Ungerleider, Martin & Haxby, 2000; Kourtzi & Kanwisher, 2001). Responsiveness of the FFA in object perception was also acknowledged before (McCarthy et al., 1997), and its activation in our experiment may possibly also be explained by the role of the FFA as a face-like overall configuration detector (Tong et al., 2000).
